I. Introduction to Proteins as Biomolecules

Proteins are large, complex biomolecules that play critical roles in the structure, function, and regulation of the body's tissues and organs.

Key Characteristics of Proteins:

  1. Proteins are polymers made up of amino acid monomers.
  2. They contain carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, and sometimes sulfur.
  3. Proteins have diverse functions including structural support, catalysis, transport, and defense.
  4. Each protein has a unique three-dimensional structure that determines its function.
  5. Proteins can be denatured by changes in temperature, pH, or chemical environment.

Biological Importance: Proteins are involved in virtually all cellular processes and are essential for life. They make up about 50% of the dry weight of most cells.

C. Essential vs. Non-essential Amino Acids

Essential amino acids cannot be synthesized by the human body and must be obtained from the diet. Non-essential amino acids can be synthesized by the body.

Essential Amino Acids (9): Histidine, Isoleucine, Leucine, Lysine, Methionine, Phenylalanine, Threonine, Tryptophan, Valine

Non-essential Amino Acids (11): Alanine, Arginine, Asparagine, Aspartic acid, Cysteine, Glutamic acid, Glutamine, Glycine, Proline, Serine, Tyrosine

III. Peptide Bond Formation and Characteristics

A. Formation of Peptide Bonds

A peptide bond is a covalent chemical bond formed between two amino acid molecules when the carboxyl group of one amino acid reacts with the amino group of another, releasing a molecule of water (H‚ÇÇO).

Peptide Bond Formation

Formation of a peptide bond through dehydration synthesis

This reaction is a condensation reaction (dehydration synthesis) because it releases a water molecule.

B. Characteristics of Peptide Bonds

Key Properties

  • Peptide bonds are strong covalent bonds with partial double bond character.
  • They are planar and rigid, restricting rotation around the bond.
  • The peptide bond has a trans configuration in most cases, minimizing steric hindrance.
  • Peptide bonds can be hydrolyzed by strong acids, bases, or specific enzymes called proteases.

C. Polypeptides and Proteins

A polypeptide is a chain of amino acids linked by peptide bonds. A protein consists of one or more polypeptides folded into a specific three-dimensional structure.

Terminology:

  • Dipeptide: 2 amino acids joined by 1 peptide bond
  • Tripeptide: 3 amino acids joined by 2 peptide bonds
  • Oligopeptide: 4-20 amino acids
  • Polypeptide: More than 20 amino acids
  • Protein: One or more polypeptides with a specific 3D structure

IV. Protein Structure Levels

A. Primary Structure

The primary structure of a protein is the linear sequence of amino acids in the polypeptide chain, held together by peptide bonds.

The primary structure is determined by the genetic code and dictates all higher levels of protein structure.

B. Secondary Structure

The secondary structure refers to local folded structures that form within a polypeptide due to hydrogen bonding between atoms of the backbone.

Alpha-Helix (Œ±-helix)

  • A coiled, spring-like structure stabilized by hydrogen bonds between every fourth amino acid.
  • Hydrogen bonds form between the carbonyl oxygen of one amino acid and the amino hydrogen of another.
  • Side chains extend outward from the helix.
  • Common in fibrous proteins like keratin and in many globular proteins.

Beta-Pleated Sheet (Œ≤-sheet)

  • Formed by adjacent regions of the polypeptide chain lying side by side.
  • Stabilized by hydrogen bonds between carbonyl and amino groups of adjacent strands.
  • Can be parallel (same direction) or antiparallel (opposite directions).
  • Found in silk fibroin and many globular proteins.

C. Tertiary Structure

The tertiary structure is the overall three-dimensional shape of a single polypeptide chain, resulting from interactions between side chains (R groups) of amino acids.

Stabilizing interactions include:

  • Hydrophobic interactions: Nonpolar side chains cluster together away from water.
  • Hydrogen bonds: Between polar side chains.
  • Ionic bonds: Between positively and negatively charged side chains.
  • Disulfide bridges: Covalent bonds between cysteine residues.
  • Van der Waals forces: Weak attractions between adjacent atoms.

D. Quaternary Structure

The quaternary structure is the arrangement of multiple polypeptide chains (subunits) in a protein complex.

Not all proteins have quaternary structure - only those with multiple polypeptide chains.

Examples:

  • Hemoglobin: 4 subunits (2 alpha chains, 2 beta chains)
  • Collagen: 3 polypeptide chains twisted into a triple helix
  • Antibodies: 4 polypeptide chains (2 heavy, 2 light)

V. Functions of Proteins in Living Organisms

A. Structural Proteins

Structural proteins provide support and shape to cells, tissues, and organs.

Examples:

  • Collagen: In connective tissues, tendons, and bones
  • Keratin: In hair, nails, and skin
  • Elastin: In elastic tissues like blood vessels
  • Actin and Myosin: In muscle contraction

B. Enzymes

Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up chemical reactions in cells.

Examples:

  • Amylase: Breaks down starch
  • Pepsin: Digests proteins in the stomach
  • DNA polymerase: Synthesizes DNA
  • Catalase: Breaks down hydrogen peroxide

C. Transport Proteins

Transport proteins carry substances throughout the body or across cell membranes.

Examples:

  • Hemoglobin: Transports oxygen in blood
  • Serum albumin: Transports fatty acids in blood
  • Membrane transporters: Move ions and molecules across cell membranes

D. Defense Proteins

Defense proteins protect the body against disease and foreign invaders.

Examples:

  • Antibodies (Immunoglobulins): Recognize and neutralize pathogens
  • Fibrinogen: Forms blood clots to prevent bleeding
  • Complement proteins: Part of the immune system

E. Regulatory Proteins

Regulatory proteins control cellular processes and physiological functions.

Examples:

  • Hormones: Insulin, glucagon, growth hormone
  • Transcription factors: Regulate gene expression
  • Receptor proteins: Receive chemical signals

F. Other Functions

Additional protein functions:

  • Storage: Ferritin stores iron, casein stores amino acids in milk
  • Motor proteins: Myosin, kinesin, dynein for movement
  • Signaling: Cytokines, growth factors
  • Toxins: Snake venoms, bacterial toxins

VI. Protein Denaturation and Factors Affecting It

A. Protein Denaturation

Denaturation is the process in which a protein loses its native three-dimensional structure, resulting in the loss of its biological function.

Denaturation involves the disruption of:

  • Secondary structure (breaking hydrogen bonds)
  • Tertiary structure (disrupting interactions between R groups)
  • Quaternary structure (separating subunits)

Important Note: Denaturation affects the protein's 3D structure but does not break peptide bonds. The primary structure remains intact.

B. Factors Causing Denaturation

Temperature

High temperatures increase molecular motion, disrupting weak interactions that stabilize protein structure.

Example: Cooking an egg causes the egg white proteins to denature and coagulate.

pH Changes

Extreme pH levels alter the charge on amino acid side chains, disrupting ionic bonds and hydrogen bonding.

Example: Stomach acid (low pH) denatures dietary proteins, making them easier to digest.

Organic Solvents

Solvents like alcohol and acetone disrupt hydrophobic interactions by interacting with nonpolar R groups.

Example: Alcohol is used as a disinfectant because it denatures bacterial proteins.

Heavy Metal Ions

Metal ions like lead, mercury, and silver can bind to protein functional groups, disrupting structure.

Example: Mercury poisoning occurs because mercury ions denature essential enzymes.

Mechanical Agitation

Vigorous shaking or stirring can disrupt protein structure.

Example: Whipping egg whites introduces air bubbles and denatures proteins, forming a foam.

C. Renaturation

Renaturation is the process by which a denatured protein regains its native structure and function when returned to normal conditions.

Not all proteins can renature. Some denaturation is irreversible, especially when it involves breaking covalent bonds or extensive aggregation.
